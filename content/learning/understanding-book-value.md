## What is Book Value?

Book value is the net asset value of a company, calculated as total assets minus total liabilities. In simpler terms, it is what the company would be worth if it were liquidated today, with all assets sold at their book values and all liabilities paid off. Book value represents the shareholders' residual claim on the company's assets after all debts have been settled. For individual shareholders, book value per share is calculated by dividing the total book value by the number of outstanding shares.

Book value is an accounting concept and is derived directly from the balance sheet. For Nepali companies, book value includes tangible assets like land, buildings, machinery, cash, investments, and receivables, minus all borrowings, payables, and other obligations. It is important to understand that book value is based on historical cost accounting, meaning assets are recorded at their purchase price minus depreciation, not at their current market value. This is a critical distinction because many Nepali companies own prime real estate that was purchased decades ago at a fraction of its current market value, but this appreciation is not reflected in the book value.

> **Book value is like the replacement cost of a company.** It tells you what the company's net assets are worth on paper, but it may significantly understate or overstate the true economic value of the business.

## What is Market Price?

Market price is simply the price at which a stock is currently trading on NEPSE. It is determined by the forces of supply and demand among buyers and sellers in the market. The market price reflects the collective opinion of all market participants about the future earning potential, growth prospects, risk profile, and competitive position of the company. Unlike book value, which is backward-looking, the market price is forward-looking and incorporates expectations about the future.

## The Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio

The Price-to-Book ratio is the primary metric used to compare a stock's market price with its book value. It is calculated by dividing the current market price per share by the book value per share.

- **P/B Formula:** Market Price / Book Value Per Share
- **P/B > 1.0:** Market expects growth
- **P/B = 1.0:** Market values assets at book
- **P/B < 1.0:** Stock at discount to book

## When P/B Below 1.0 is a Bargain

A stock trading below book value (P/B less than 1.0) can represent a genuine buying opportunity under certain conditions:

- When the company has strong and growing earnings, a low P/B suggests the stock is significantly undervalued.
- When the company's assets include appreciating real estate or investments carried at historical cost.
- When a temporary crisis has depressed the stock price but the underlying business remains viable.
- When the company is a market leader with a strong competitive position.
- When the company has a consistent history of profitability and dividend payments.

## When P/B Below 1.0 is a Value Trap

Not every stock trading below book value is a bargain. Some are value traps:

- When the company has been reporting losses for several consecutive quarters.
- When the company's assets include obsolete inventory, uncollectible receivables, or impaired investments.
- When the company operates in a sunset industry with structural headwinds.
- When management has a track record of poor capital allocation.
- When the company has high leverage and the debt servicing burden is consuming cash flow.

## Using P/B Ratio in Practice

The P/B ratio is most useful when comparing similar companies within the same sector. On NEPSE, commercial banks typically trade at 1.5-3.0x P/B, hydropower companies at 2.0-4.0x P/B, and manufacturing companies show the widest range from 0.5x to 5.0x P/B.

> **Combine P/B with ROE:** The most powerful use of the P/B ratio is in combination with Return on Equity (ROE). A company with high ROE (above 15%) deserves a higher P/B ratio. If you find a company with high ROE trading at a low P/B, you may have found an undervalued stock.

## Conclusion

The gap between book value and market price is one of the most informative signals in equity analysis. Understanding when a low P/B ratio represents a genuine opportunity versus a value trap is a skill that separates successful investors from those who chase cheap stocks without understanding the underlying fundamentals. Always combine the P/B ratio with other metrics like ROE, earnings growth, and debt levels.
