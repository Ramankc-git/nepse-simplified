  ## Sector Overview

  The Hydropower sector on NEPSE has undergone a dramatic transformation over the past two decades, evolving from just 3 listed companies in 2007 to 97 listed companies by 2026. This explosive growth reflects Nepal's natural endowment of over 42,000 MW of commercially viable hydropower potential, of which less than 3% has been harnessed so far. The sector now represents one of the largest segments on the Nepal Stock Exchange by number of listed entities and continues to attract significant investor interest as the country pursues its energy ambitions.

  The NEPSE Hydropower sub-index stood at 3,818.39 as of May 14, 2026, recording a modest decline of 0.26% on the day. However, on a broader scale, the sector has shown mixed performance throughout 2026, influenced by factors including regulatory uncertainty around power purchase agreements, interest rate movements, and the pace of project commissioning. Within the sector, 8 stocks gained on the day of the latest trading session, indicating selective buying interest despite the overall mild weakness.

  **Hydropower Index:** 3,818.39 (-0.26% on May 14, 2026)

  The hydropower sector's performance must be viewed in the context of the broader NEPSE index, which closed at 2,730.17 on May 14, 2026, down 10.07 points or 0.36%. The fact that the hydro index declined by only 0.26% against the broader market's 0.36% decline suggests relative resilience — investors are not aggressively selling hydro stocks even as the overall market faces headwinds.

  The sector's performance has been characterized by a bifurcation between premium companies like Chilime Hydropower (CHCL) and Butwal Power Company (BPCL), which trade at significant premiums to book value, and the larger universe of smaller companies that often trade below par value due to execution risk and delayed project timelines. This divergence has widened in 2026 as investors favor quality and track record over speculative project pipelines.

  ## Key Players and Company Analysis

  ### Chilime Hydropower Company Limited (CHCL)

  Chilime Hydropower is the undisputed leader in the NEPSE hydropower sector, consistently posting the highest profit margins and maintaining the strongest balance sheet among listed hydro companies. As per the latest available data, Chilime has maintained its lead in reserve and surplus at Rs. 4.42 Arba, which is exceptional for a hydropower company of its scale. The company operates the 22.1 MW Chilime Hydropower Project in Rasuwa district and has been a reliable dividend payer with consistent cash returns to shareholders.

  Chilime's premium valuation reflects its proven track record of project execution, stable cash flows from long-term power purchase agreements (PPAs) with the Nepal Electricity Authority (NEA), and a conservative capital structure with minimal debt. The company serves as a benchmark for the entire sector — when Chilime moves, the rest of the hydro sector tends to follow.

  ### Butwal Power Company Limited (BPCL)

  Butwal Power Company is another established name in the sector, operating multiple hydropower projects and maintaining a diversified revenue stream. With a market capitalization of approximately NPR 23.72 billion and a paid-up capital of NPR 3.41 billion, BPCL is among the larger hydro companies by market value. The company has a long operating history since its listing on December 28, 2004, and has delivered consistent returns to shareholders through both dividend payments and capital appreciation.

  BPCL's portfolio approach to hydropower development — operating multiple projects of varying sizes — provides natural diversification against single-project risk. This is a critical advantage in the Nepalese hydropower context where projects often face delays due to geological challenges, regulatory hurdles, and financing constraints.

  ### Sector Composition

  The hydropower sector on NEPSE now includes a diverse range of companies at various stages of development. At the premium end are established operators like CHCL, BPCL, and a handful of others with operational projects and proven revenue streams. In the middle tier are companies with projects under construction that are nearing commissioning. At the speculative end are companies that are still in the pre-construction phase, dependent on regulatory approvals and financing arrangements.

  > **Tip — Understanding Project Risk:** When evaluating a hydropower stock, always check whether the company's projects are operational, under construction, or merely proposed. The gap between a project's proposed capacity and actual generation can be significant, and most stock price corrections in this sector occur when companies fail to meet construction timelines.

  ## Macroeconomic Drivers

  Several structural factors support the long-term investment thesis for Nepal's hydropower sector, even as short-term headwinds persist.

  ### Energy Demand and Export Potential

  Nepal's domestic electricity demand continues to grow at 8-10% annually, driven by urbanization, industrialization, and rural electrification programs. More significantly, the prospect of exporting surplus power to India through the upgraded transmission infrastructure has opened a massive revenue opportunity for Nepalese hydropower companies. The power export agreements with India, negotiated at competitive tariffs, provide a viable offtake channel that was previously unavailable, particularly for companies that can demonstrate reliable generation capacity.

  ### Government Policy Support

  The Government of Nepal has consistently identified hydropower development as a national priority, with targets to develop 15,000 MW of capacity by 2030. While progress against these targets has been slower than announced, the policy framework remains supportive with tax incentives, subsidized financing through development banks, and streamlined licensing procedures for qualifying projects. The recent focus on attracting foreign direct investment into the sector is another positive signal.

  ### Interest Rate Environment

  The cost of debt financing remains a critical factor for the hydropower sector, as most companies rely heavily on bank loans to fund construction. Nepal Rastra Bank's current tight monetary policy, with policy rates remaining elevated to control inflation, has increased borrowing costs and squeezed project returns. However, there are indications that NRB may begin easing monetary policy in the second half of FY 2082/83, which would significantly benefit the sector by reducing interest expenses on construction loans and improving project economics.

  ## Risks and Challenges

  Despite the strong long-term narrative, the hydropower sector faces several material risks that investors must carefully consider.

  ### Project Execution Risk

  The single largest risk for most hydropower companies is the inability to complete projects on time and within budget. Geological surprises during tunnel construction, delays in forest clearance and environmental permits, local community disputes, and supply chain disruptions for electromechanical equipment are common challenges. Companies with a track record of on-time delivery (like Chilime) command premium valuations precisely because this risk is priced into lower-tier stocks.

  ### Regulatory and Offtake Risk

  The power purchase agreement (PPA) framework with the Nepal Electricity Authority remains the primary revenue mechanism for most hydropower companies. Changes in PPA terms, including the shift from fixed-rate to variable-rate PPAs for new projects, have reduced the guaranteed returns available to new developers. Additionally, NEA's own financial constraints have occasionally led to payment delays, creating working capital challenges for hydro companies.

  ### Overcapacity Concerns

  With 97 listed hydropower companies and many more projects in the pipeline, there is a growing risk of overcapacity in the domestic market. If generation capacity significantly outpaces domestic demand growth and power export arrangements do not scale as projected, downward pressure on electricity tariffs could compress margins across the sector. This is a medium-term risk that investors should monitor closely.

  ## Investment Strategy

  For investors looking at the hydropower sector, a differentiated approach is essential given the wide dispersion in company quality within the sector.

  ### Quality-Focused Approach

  The most prudent strategy is to focus on established operators with operational projects, strong balance sheets, and proven management teams. Companies like Chilime (CHCL) and Butwal Power (BPCL) offer more predictable cash flows and lower downside risk. While their valuations may appear rich on traditional metrics like P/E or P/B, the premium is justified by their lower execution risk and consistent dividend payments.

  ### Growth Opportunity Approach

  For investors with higher risk tolerance, companies with projects in advanced stages of construction (near commissioning) offer an attractive risk-reward profile. These companies trade at lower valuations but stand to experience significant earnings jumps once their projects begin commercial generation. The key is to identify companies that are within 6-12 months of commissioning and have secured both financing and regulatory approvals.

  ### Sectors to Watch

  - **Premium Tier:** CHCL, BPCL — stable earnings, high valuations, suitable for conservative investors
  - **Growth Tier:** Companies nearing project completion — higher risk but meaningful upside potential
  - **Speculative Tier:** Pre-construction companies — only for high-risk investors who can accept total capital loss

  ## Outlook

  The NEPSE Hydropower sector is at a pivotal juncture. The near-term outlook is cautiously neutral, weighed down by high interest rates, regulatory uncertainty, and a broader market downtrend that has seen NEPSE fall from the 2,800+ range to 2,719 in recent sessions. However, the medium to long-term fundamentals remain compelling, driven by Nepal's vast untapped hydro potential and the transformative opportunity of power export to India.

  Investors should maintain exposure to quality hydropower stocks while being patient on entry points. The sector tends to perform well in anticipation of monetary policy easing and ahead of the monsoon season when generation peaks. A phased allocation strategy — buying on weakness with a 12-24 month investment horizon — remains the most appropriate approach for most investor profiles.

The sector's long-term growth potential is intact, but near-term volatility should be expected. Focus on companies with operational projects, avoid pre-construction speculation, and maintain realistic return expectations of 15-25% annually for well-selected positions.
